Fish Oil Supplements questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Fish oil and krill oil supplements provide essential omega-3s, but they differ in absorption, purity, and sustainability. Choosing the right product depends on your health goals, budget, and dietary needs. Look for third-party tested supplements with clear EPA and DHA amounts.

What is the difference between fish oil and krill oil?

Fish oil comes from fatty fish like sardines, anchovies, and mackerel. It typically delivers more EPA and DHA per serving, and at a lower cost, than krill oil. Krill oil, sourced from tiny shrimp-like crustaceans, contains omega-3s in phospholipid form, which some research suggests may be absorbed more easily. It also naturally contains astaxanthin, a potent antioxidant that helps prevent the oil from going rancid. However, krill oil is generally more expensive and yields lower EPA and DHA per capsule, meaning you may need to take more to reach a therapeutic dose.

How much omega-3 do you really need per day?

There is no single official daily recommendation for omega-3s, but major health organizations suggest a combined intake of 250–500 mg of EPA and DHA daily for general health. For specific conditions like high triglycerides or inflammatory issues, studies often use 2,000–4,000 mg per day under medical supervision. Pay attention to the labeled amounts of EPA and DHA, not the total oil content. Many bargain supplements list ‘fish oil 1,000 mg’ but only deliver 300 mg of actual omega-3s. Aim for a product that clearly states how much EPA and DHA you are getting per serving.

What should you look for on a fish oil label?

Start with the EPA and DHA numbers, these are the active omega-3s you are paying for. A good standard is at least 500 mg combined per softgel, but many concentrated formulas deliver 700–1,000 mg. Next, check for third-party testing seals from USP, NSF, or IFOS (International Fish Oil Standards). These confirm the product is free of heavy metals, PCBs, and dioxins, and that the labeled potency is accurate. Also look for the source: small, oily fish like anchovies and sardines are preferable because they are lower on the food chain and accumulate fewer contaminants. Avoid products with vague language like ‘wild fish blend’ without specifying species.

Does the form matter: liquid vs. softgels vs. gummies?

Liquids are the most cost-effective way to get a high dose of omega-3s, but they can have a fishy taste unless flavored. Softgels are convenient and mask odor well, though some people find them hard to swallow. Gummies are palatable but typically contain very low amounts of EPA and DHA. often under 100 mg per piece, and may have added sugar. For anyone serious about meeting a therapeutic dose, softgels or liquid are usually the better choices. If you opt for gummies, be prepared to take several to approach meaningful levels, and watch for sugar content.

Are there side effects or risks with fish oil supplements?

Fish oil is generally safe for most people, but high doses can cause minor side effects like fishy burps, loose stools, or nausea. Taking the supplement with meals and choosing enteric-coated softgels can reduce these issues. A more serious concern is blood thinning: omega-3s at doses above 3 grams daily may prolong bleeding time. If you take blood thinners or have a bleeding disorder, talk to your doctor before starting high-dose fish oil. Also, because fish oil is prone to oxidation, store it in a cool, dark place, or refrigerate liquids, and always check for a rancid smell before use.

How do you choose a reputable brand?

Stick with brands that voluntarily submit their products for third-party testing and publicly share results. Companies like Nordic Naturals, Carlson Labs, Now Foods, and Thorne Research have long track records of transparency and quality. Avoid brands that make vague health claims like “cardiovascular support” without listing specific clinical studies or dosages. Price is not always an indicator of quality: some affordable brands still pass international purity standards. The best bet is to cross-reference the product name with IFOS or ConsumerLab reports to see if it has passed independent certification.

Frequently asked questions

Can fish oil help with joint pain?

Some research suggests that omega-3s can reduce inflammation and may ease symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis and general joint stiffness. Results vary by person, and it is not a substitute for prescribed medication. A daily dose of at least 2,000 mg of EPA and DHA, taken consistently for several months, is often needed to notice a difference.

Is it safe to take fish oil every day?

Yes, for most healthy adults, daily fish oil is safe at doses up to 3,000 mg of combined EPA and DHA. Higher amounts should be taken only under medical supervision. Stick to reputable brands and store the oil properly to avoid rancidity.

What is the best time to take fish oil?

Taking fish oil with a meal that contains some fat, like breakfast or dinner, improves absorption and reduces the likelihood of fishy burps. Splitting your dose across two meals can also help if you are taking a high amount.

Do I need to refrigerate fish oil?

Refrigeration is recommended for liquid fish oils once opened, as cold slows down oxidation. Softgels do not require refrigeration but should be kept in a cool, dark cupboard. Avoid leaving any fish oil supplement in a hot car or near a stove.

Can fish oil go bad?

Yes, fish oil can oxidize and become rancid, which not only smells unpleasant but may also reduce effectiveness and potentially increase inflammation. Always check for a strong, sour fishy odor or a bitter taste. Most bottles have an expiration date, but once opened, use within three months.

Should I take fish oil if I'm on blood thinners?

If you take anticoagulant medications like warfarin or apixaban, consult your doctor before starting fish oil. High doses of omega-3s can add to the blood-thinning effect and raise the risk of bruising or bleeding. Your provider can help you decide on a safe dose or an alternative.

In shortChoosing the right omega-3 supplement boils down to knowing your health priorities, reading labels with a critical eye, and opting for a product that is third-party tested for purity and potency. Both fish oil and krill oil have merits, but consistency matters more than the source. Start with a moderate dose, listen to your body, and adjust based on your goals and any professional guidance you receive.
